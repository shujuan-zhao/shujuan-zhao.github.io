Our recent publication in *Nature Communications* represents a major breakthrough in understanding the genetic architecture of Vein of Galen Malformations (VOGM), the most severe congenital brain arteriovenous malformation affecting newborns.

## The Challenge

VOGM occurs in approximately 1 in 60,000 births and can lead to:
- High-output cardiac failure in neonates
- Severe neurological damage
- Mortality rates exceeding 50% without intervention

Despite its severity, the genetic causes remained unknown, limiting our ability to develop targeted therapies beyond surgical intervention.

## Our Approach

We assembled and analyzed the world's largest VOGM cohort:
- **310 patient families** underwent whole-exome sequencing
- **336,326 single-cell transcriptomes** from developing human brain vasculature
- **Functional validation** in zebrafish and mouse models

## Key Findings

1. **RASA1 mutations**: We identified a 2042.5-fold enrichment of de novo loss-of-function variants in RASA1 (p=4.79×10⁻⁷)
2. **EPHB4 involvement**: Rare damaging variants showed 17.5-fold enrichment (p=1.22×10⁻⁵)
3. **Cellular origin**: Single-cell analysis pinpointed developing endothelial cells as the disease locus
4. **Therapeutic target**: All implicated genes converge on the MAPK signaling pathway

## Impact

This work transforms VOGM from a poorly understood surgical emergency to a genetic disorder with clear molecular targets. The identification of the MAPK pathway opens doors for potential pharmacological interventions that could complement or even replace high-risk surgical procedures.

Our findings also have broader implications for understanding brain vascular development and related disorders like arteriovenous malformations in adults.
