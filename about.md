## Shujuan Zhao
Ph.D. Candidate in Molecular Genetics and Genomics  
Washington University in St. Louis School of Medicine

I am a doctoral candidate working at the intersection of human genetics, computational biology, and translational medicine. My research focuses on unraveling the genetic and molecular mechanisms underlying rare congenital disorders, particularly neurovascular and cardiovascular diseases.

### Research Focus

My doctoral work centers on **Vein of Galen Malformations (VOGM)**, the most severe type of congenital brain arteriovenous malformation in neonates. Leading the genomic analysis of the world's largest VOGM patient cohort (310 families), I identified key genetic mutations in the RASA1/EPHB4 pathway, work that was published as first author in [Nature Communications](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-023-43062-z).

### Research Interests

**1. Molecular Mechanisms of Gene Regulation**  
I investigate the complex processes that control gene expression and interaction at the molecular level, with particular focus on how disruptions in these mechanisms lead to developmental disorders.

**2. Genomic Dark Matter**  
I explore undercharacterized regions of the genome, including non-coding elements and their potential regulatory roles in human disease.

**3. Genome-Editing Technologies**  
I am passionate about advancing and applying cutting-edge genome-editing tools (CRISPR, base editing, prime editing) for both research discovery and therapeutic development.

### Approach

My research philosophy emphasizes:
- **Integrative Analysis**: Combining whole-exome sequencing with single-cell transcriptomics to understand disease mechanisms at unprecedented resolution
- **Functional Validation**: Moving beyond statistical associations to prove causality through experiments in zebrafish and mouse models
- **Translational Impact**: Bridging genetic discoveries to potential therapeutic targets, informed by my background in pharmaceutical engineering

### Background

My unique journey from pharmaceutical engineering and organic chemistry to human genetics provides me with a holistic perspective on therapeutic development—from target discovery to drug design. This interdisciplinary foundation enables me to approach complex biological problems with both computational rigor and translational insight.
