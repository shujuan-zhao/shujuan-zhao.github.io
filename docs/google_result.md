Her proven ability to drive complex research projects from initial genetic discovery to deep mechanistic insight, coupled with her exceptional publication record and translational mindset, positions her as a premier candidate for leading roles in both academic and industrial research environments.Research Trajectory and Core Scientific ContributionsDr. Shujuan Zhao's research portfolio demonstrates a clear and cohesive intellectual trajectory focused on understanding the fundamental genetic mechanisms that govern human development and how their disruption leads to disease. Her work is thematically centered on congenital disorders of the neuro-cardiovascular axis, a focus that has allowed her to develop profound domain expertise in the signaling pathways and transcriptional programs essential for the proper formation of the brain, heart, and vasculature.Doctoral Thesis Focus: Defining the Genetic Landscape of Vein of Galen Malformations (VOGM)The centerpiece of Dr. Zhao's doctoral research is her comprehensive investigation into the genetic basis of Vein of Galen Malformations (VOGM), the most common and severe type of congenital brain arteriovenous malformation diagnosed in neonates.3 These malformations, which involve direct connections between cerebral arteries and the precursor to the vein of Galen, can lead to catastrophic outcomes, including high-output congestive heart failure and severe brain damage.3 The lack of understanding of VOGM's genetic etiology has significantly hindered the development of non-procedural, targeted therapies, creating a critical unmet medical need.Recognizing this gap, Dr. Zhao assumed a leadership role in this ambitious research project in 2020.3 A key challenge in studying rare diseases is the assembly of sufficiently powered patient cohorts. Dr. Zhao was instrumental in the analysis of a cohort of 310 VOGM proband-family exomes, which stands as the world's largest genetic study of this condition to date.3 This undertaking alone highlights her remarkable initiative, persistence, and project management capabilities.Her scientific approach to this complex problem was not limited to a single modality but was a sophisticated, integrative strategy designed to move from statistical association to biological mechanism. This multi-pronged strategy involved:Large-Scale Human Genomics: The core of the study was the analysis of whole-exome sequencing (WES) data from the 310 VOGM probands and their families. This large-scale effort was essential for achieving the statistical power needed to identify rare, causative genetic variants with confidence.5Multi-Omics Integration: Dr. Zhao went beyond standard exome analysis by integrating data from 336,326 human cerebrovasculature single-cell transcriptomes. This innovative step allowed her to overlay genetic findings onto a high-resolution cellular map of the developing brain's vasculature, enabling the identification of the likely spatio-temporal origin of VOGM pathophysiology. Her analysis pointed specifically to developing endothelial cells as a key cellular locus.3Rigorous Functional Validation: A defining feature of Dr. Zhao's scientific philosophy is her commitment to experimental validation. As she noted, "it's very significant that we included functional studies".3 She collaborated with experts to validate the functional consequences of the identified genetic variants using multiple model systems. This included in vivo experiments in both mouse models, conducted with collaborators at the University of Michigan, and zebrafish models at the Yale Zebrafish Core.3 This crucial step moves the research from correlation to causation, providing the biological proof necessary to solidify the genetic findings.This comprehensive investigation yielded several landmark discoveries, published with Dr. Zhao as the first author in Nature Communications.4 Key findings include:Identification of RASA1: The study found a genome-wide significant burden of de novo loss-of-function variants in the Ras suppressor gene RASA1 (2042.5-fold enrichment, p=4.79×10−7), firmly establishing it as a key VOGM risk gene.5Implication of EPHB4: The analysis also uncovered a significant enrichment of rare, damaging transmitted variants in the Ephrin receptor-B4 gene (EPHB4) (17.5-fold enrichment, p=1.22×10−5). EPHB4 is known to cooperate with RASA1 in regulating vascular development, revealing a critical genetic pathway underlying VOGM.5Therapeutic Pathway Discovery: The convergence of these and other implicated genes (e.g., ACVRL1, NOTCH1, PTPN11) on the MAPK signaling pathway strongly suggests that this pathway is a central driver of the disease and represents a promising potential target for future therapeutic strategies.3This body of work forms the basis of her Ph.D. thesis, titled "Investigating the Genetics and Genomics of Vein of Galen Malformations."
